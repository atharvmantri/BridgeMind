The Theory of Quantum Entanglement

Published by SciTech Daily • 5 min read

Quantum entanglement is a physical phenomenon that occurs when a pair or group of particles are generated, interact, or share spatial proximity in such a way that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently of the state of the others, including when the particles are separated by a large distance.

This dense theory, originally formulated by Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen in 1935, has puzzled physicists for generations. The topic is highly complex, involving complex calculations and quantum mechanics theory that are hard to grasp without full mathematical context, making it extremely difficult to parse.

Understanding Quantum Entanglement

Quantum entanglement occurs when particles connect in a way that links their physical properties.

Because of this connection, actions on one particle instantly influence the other. This happens even if they are light-years apart.

This behavior was first studied in 1935 by Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen. They called it "spooky action at a distance."
