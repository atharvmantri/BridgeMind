Introduction to Quantum Computing

Pre-loaded Article • Encyclopedia excerpt

Quantum computing is a rapidly-emerging technology that harnesses the laws of quantum mechanics to solve problems too complex for classical computers. These machines are fundamentally different from the classical computers we use today. Classical computers process information in bits, which represent either a 0 or a 1. Quantum computers, however, use quantum bits, or qubits, which can exist in a state of superposition.

Superposition is a quantum principle that allows a qubit to represent a 0, a 1, or any quantum proportion of both simultaneously. Because qubits can exist in superposition, a quantum computer can process vast amounts of calculations in parallel. Another critical principle of quantum mechanics used in quantum computing is quantum entanglement.

Quantum entanglement describes a close relationship between particles where the state of one particle instantly correlates with the state of another, even when separated by astronomical distances. Einstein famously referred to this phenomenon as "spooky action at a distance." In quantum computing, entangled qubits allow qubits to share information instantly, accelerating computational speed exponentially.

To realize these computational gains, quantum algorithms exploit entanglement and superposition to manipulate probability amplitudes. Major applications include quantum cryptography (which could break RSA encryption while securing communication networks), simulation of complex chemical molecular reactions for drug discovery, and logistics optimization modeling.
